What Is Investing

Investing means putting money into assets with the expectation of growing it over time. Unlike saving, which protects wealth, investing aims to build it.

  • Beats inflation
  • Builds long-term wealth
  • Enables financial independence

Example: $1,000 invested in the S&P 500 20 years ago could be worth over $6,000 today.

Stocks: Ownership in a company. Profit comes from price increases and dividends.

Bonds: Loans to companies or governments that pay interest back over time.

Mutual Funds & ETFs: Pools of investments. ETFs track indexes and trade like stocks. Mutual funds are actively managed.

Index Funds: Low-cost funds that track the whole market, like the S&P 500. Great for beginners.

Real Estate: Property or REITs. Provides rental income and appreciation.

Crypto: Digital assets like Bitcoin. High risk and high volatility. Not beginner-friendly.

  1. Open a brokerage account: Fidelity, Vanguard, or Robinhood.
  2. Choose your investments: Start with index funds or ETFs.
  3. Use dollar-cost averaging: Invest the same amount regularly.
  4. Start early: Compound growth works better with time.

Higher reward usually means higher risk. Long-term investors can ride out short-term dips.

  • Stocks = higher risk, higher reward
  • Bonds = safer, but lower returns
  • Diversify to manage risk
